Unit A

Nervous & Endocrine Systems

How the body detects, processes, and responds to stimuli through the nervous system and chemical messengers of the endocrine system.

of Diploma

General Outcome 1 — Nervous System

  • Neuron types: sensory (afferent), motor (efferent), interneuron (association)
  • Structure: dendrites → cell body → axon → axon terminals → synaptic knobs
  • Myelin sheath: Schwann cells (PNS) / oligodendrocytes (CNS); speeds transmission via saltatory conduction
  • Resting potential: −70 mV; maintained by Na⁺/K⁺ pump (3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in)
  • Action potential: depolarization (Na⁺ in) → repolarization (K⁺ out) → hyperpolarization → refractory period
  • All-or-nothing law: stimulus must reach threshold; intensity coded by frequency of APs
  • Synapse: presynaptic neuron → synaptic cleft → postsynaptic neuron
  • Neurotransmitters: acetylcholine (ACh), dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine, GABA
  • Sequence: AP arrives → Ca²⁺ enters → vesicles release NT → NT binds receptors → EPSP or IPSP
  • Summation: spatial (multiple synapses) or temporal (rapid repeated stimuli)
  • Termination: reuptake, enzymatic breakdown (e.g. AChE breaks down ACh)
  • Cerebrum lobes: frontal (motor, reasoning), parietal (sensory, spatial), temporal (hearing, memory), occipital (vision)
  • Cerebellum: balance, coordination, fine motor control
  • Brain stem: medulla oblongata (breathing, HR, BP), pons (relay), midbrain (reflexes)
  • Hypothalamus: homeostasis, links NS and endocrine system
  • Spinal cord: white matter (tracts) surrounds grey matter (cell bodies); dorsal root = sensory, ventral root = motor
  • Reflex arc: receptor → afferent → spinal cord (interneuron) → efferent → effector; bypasses brain
  • Somatic NS: voluntary control of skeletal muscle
  • Autonomic NS: involuntary control of smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, glands
  • Sympathetic: "fight or flight" — increases HR, dilates bronchioles, inhibits digestion
  • Parasympathetic: "rest and digest" — decreases HR, constricts bronchioles, stimulates digestion
  • Eye: cornea → aqueous humour → pupil/iris → lens → vitreous humour → retina (rods: light/dark; cones: colour) → optic nerve
  • Ear: outer ear → tympanic membrane → ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes) → oval window → cochlea (hair cells) → auditory nerve
  • Accommodation: ciliary muscles change lens shape for near/far focus
  • Proprioception: muscle spindles and Golgi tendon organs sense body position
  • Multiple Sclerosis: autoimmune destruction of myelin → impaired conduction
  • Parkinson's Disease: insufficient dopamine → tremors, rigidity
  • Alzheimer's: neuron death, amyloid plaques → memory loss
  • Drug effects: stimulants increase NT release; depressants enhance inhibitory NTs (e.g. alcohol → GABA)

General Outcome 2 — Endocrine System

  • Endocrine glands: ductless; secrete hormones directly into blood
  • Key glands: hypothalamus, pituitary (anterior/posterior), thyroid, parathyroid, adrenal (cortex/medulla), pancreas, gonads
  • Steroid hormones: lipid-soluble; cross membrane; bind intracellular receptors; alter gene expression (slow, long-lasting)
  • Non-steroid hormones: water-soluble; bind membrane receptors; use second messengers (cAMP); fast, short-lived response
  • Negative feedback: response reduces the original stimulus; maintains homeostasis (e.g. blood glucose, thyroid hormones)
  • Positive feedback: response amplifies the original stimulus (e.g. LH surge triggering ovulation; oxytocin during labour)
  • Hypothalamic-pituitary axis: hypothalamus releases releasing/inhibiting hormones → anterior pituitary responds → target glands release hormones → feedback to hypothalamus
  • Insulin vs. glucagon: high blood glucose → insulin (beta cells) → glycogenesis; low blood glucose → glucagon (alpha cells) → glycogenolysis
  • Type 1 Diabetes: autoimmune destruction of beta cells → no insulin production
  • Type 2 Diabetes: cells become insulin-resistant → high blood glucose
  • Hyperthyroidism: excess thyroid hormones → increased metabolic rate, weight loss, anxiety
  • Hypothyroidism: insufficient thyroid hormones → decreased metabolic rate, fatigue, weight gain

Theme: Communication & Control. Unit A explores how the body coordinates rapid responses (nervous system) and slower, sustained responses (endocrine system). Both systems work together to maintain homeostasis.

⚡ Action Potential Steps

  • Resting: −70 mV; Na⁺ channels closed
  • Depolarization: Na⁺ channels open → Na⁺ rushes in → becomes +30 mV
  • Repolarization: K⁺ channels open → K⁺ out → membrane repolarizes
  • Hyperpolarization: briefly more negative than resting
  • Refractory period: cannot fire again immediately
  • Na⁺/K⁺ pump restores resting potential

🧠 Brain Region Functions

  • Frontal lobe: voluntary movement, personality, decision-making
  • Parietal lobe: somatosensory processing, spatial awareness
  • Temporal lobe: hearing, language comprehension, memory
  • Occipital lobe: visual processing
  • Cerebellum: balance, coordination
  • Medulla: automatic functions (breathing, heart rate)

💉 Key Hormones to Know

  • Insulin (pancreas β) → lowers blood glucose
  • Glucagon (pancreas α) → raises blood glucose
  • ADH (posterior pituitary) → water reabsorption
  • TSH (anterior pituitary) → stimulates thyroid
  • Epinephrine (adrenal medulla) → fight or flight
  • Cortisol (adrenal cortex) → stress response

🔄 Negative Feedback Template

  • Stimulus: change in body variable
  • Receptor: detects change
  • Control centre: processes signal (often hypothalamus)
  • Effector: produces response
  • Response: opposes/reverses the original change
  • System returns to set point

General Outcome 1 — Reproductive Anatomy

  • Structures: testes → epididymis → vas deferens → seminal vesicles / prostate / bulbourethral gland → urethra
  • Spermatogenesis: occurs in seminiferous tubules; FSH stimulates; takes ~72 days
  • Sperm structure: head (nucleus + acrosome), midpiece (mitochondria), flagellum
  • Testosterone: produced by Leydig cells; regulated by LH; responsible for secondary sexual characteristics
  • Structures: ovaries → fallopian tubes → uterus (endometrium) → cervix → vagina
  • Oogenesis: begins before birth; primary oocytes arrested in prophase I; one mature egg per cycle
  • Ovarian follicle: primary → secondary → Graafian → ovulation → corpus luteum → corpus albicans
  • Hormones: FSH (follicle growth), LH (ovulation trigger), estrogen (follicular phase), progesterone (luteal phase)
  • Days 1–5: menstruation — endometrium sheds; low estrogen & progesterone
  • Days 6–13: follicular phase — FSH stimulates follicle; estrogen rises; endometrium thickens
  • Day 14: LH surge → ovulation
  • Days 15–28: luteal phase — corpus luteum produces progesterone; maintains endometrium
  • No fertilization: corpus luteum degenerates; progesterone drops → menstruation

General Outcome 2 — Hormonal Regulation

  • GnRH (hypothalamus) → stimulates anterior pituitary to release FSH and LH
  • FSH: stimulates follicle development (female) and sperm production (male)
  • LH: triggers ovulation and corpus luteum formation; stimulates testosterone in males
  • Estrogen: positive feedback before ovulation (LH surge); negative feedback after
  • Progesterone: maintains uterine lining; inhibits FSH and LH (negative feedback)
  • Oral contraceptives: synthetic estrogen/progesterone → suppresses FSH/LH → prevents ovulation
  • IUD: prevents implantation; may contain copper or hormones
  • IVF: egg fertilized outside body → embryo transferred to uterus
  • Artificial insemination: sperm placed directly into reproductive tract
  • Ethical considerations: embryo status, selective reduction, genetic screening

General Outcome 3 — Development & Birth

  • Fertilization: sperm penetrates egg in fallopian tube → acrosome reaction → zona pellucida penetration → cortical reaction (blocks polyspermy)
  • Cleavage: mitotic divisions without growth → morula → blastocyst
  • Blastocyst: inner cell mass (embryo) + trophoblast (placenta)
  • Implantation: day 6–10; blastocyst embeds in endometrium; hCG secreted (maintains corpus luteum)
  • Gastrulation: 3 germ layers — ectoderm (skin, nervous system), mesoderm (muscle, bone, blood), endoderm (gut, lungs)
  • Placenta: nutrient/gas exchange; maternal and fetal blood do not mix; produces progesterone after week 10
  • Extra-embryonic membranes: amnion (amniotic fluid), chorion (chorionic villi), yolk sac, allantois
  • Critical periods: weeks 3–8; teratogens most dangerous during organogenesis
  • Teratogens: alcohol, tobacco, drugs, certain medications, radiation
  • Parturition: triggered by fetal cortisol → estrogen rise → oxytocin positive feedback loop → uterine contractions
  • Stages of labour: dilation (longest), expulsion (birth), placental stage
  • Lactation: prolactin (anterior pituitary) → milk production; oxytocin (posterior pituitary) → milk let-down reflex

Theme: Continuity of Life. Unit B explores how humans reproduce and how a single cell develops into a fully formed human being. Hormonal feedback loops coordinate the entire process.

📈 Menstrual Cycle Hormones

  • Day 1–5: FSH rises → follicle grows → estrogen low
  • Day 6–13: estrogen rises → endometrium thickens
  • Day 13–14: estrogen peaks → LH surge → ovulation
  • Day 15–28: corpus luteum → progesterone peaks → endometrium maintained
  • Day 28: progesterone/estrogen drop → menstruation

🌱 Embryonic Development Timeline

  • Day 0: fertilization in fallopian tube
  • Day 3–4: morula (solid ball of cells)
  • Day 5–6: blastocyst forms
  • Day 6–10: implantation in endometrium
  • Weeks 3–8: embryonic period; organogenesis
  • Week 9+: fetal period; growth and maturation

🔄 Key Hormones Summary

  • FSH: follicle/sperm development
  • LH: ovulation trigger; testosterone stimulation
  • Estrogen: follicular phase; secondary sex chars
  • Progesterone: luteal phase; maintains pregnancy
  • hCG: from trophoblast; maintains corpus luteum
  • Oxytocin: labour contractions; milk let-down

🏥 Germ Layer Derivatives

  • Ectoderm → skin, nervous system, sense organs
  • Mesoderm → muscle, bone, blood, connective tissue, kidneys
  • Endoderm → digestive tract, respiratory system, liver, pancreas
  • All three germ layers form during gastrulation (week 3)

Unit C

Cell Division, Genetics & Molecular Biology

How cells divide, how traits are inherited, and how DNA carries, expresses, and passes on genetic information.

Unit C Progress

Cell Division (3A)
0%

Section 3A — Cell Division

  • Interphase: G1 (growth), S (DNA replication), G2 (preparation for division)
  • Prophase: chromosomes condense; spindle forms; nuclear envelope breaks down
  • Metaphase: chromosomes align at metaphase plate; spindle fibres attach to centromeres
  • Anaphase: sister chromatids separate; pulled to opposite poles
  • Telophase + Cytokinesis: nuclear envelopes reform; cytoplasm divides
  • Result: 2 identical diploid daughter cells (2n → 2n)
  • Meiosis I: homologous chromosomes separate (reduction division) — crossing over occurs in prophase I
  • Meiosis II: sister chromatids separate (like mitosis)
  • Result: 4 haploid daughter cells (2n → n); each genetically unique
  • Crossing over: exchange of segments between homologous chromosomes → genetic recombination
  • Non-disjunction: chromosomes fail to separate → aneuploidy (monosomy, trisomy)
  • Karyotype: ordered arrangement of chromosome pairs by size and shape
  • Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21): non-disjunction → extra chromosome 21
  • Turner Syndrome (45, X0): monosomy of sex chromosome; female phenotype
  • Klinefelter Syndrome (47, XXY): extra X in male; reduced fertility
  • Polyploidy: more than two complete sets of chromosomes; important in plant evolution

Section 3B — Mendelian Genetics

  • Mendel's Laws: Segregation (alleles separate during gamete formation); Independent Assortment (genes on different chromosomes assort independently)
  • Monohybrid cross: Aa × Aa → 1:2:1 genotype ratio; 3:1 phenotype ratio
  • Dihybrid cross: AaBb × AaBb → 9:3:3:1 phenotype ratio
  • Incomplete dominance: heterozygote has intermediate phenotype (e.g. RR red × WW white → RW pink)
  • Codominance: both alleles expressed simultaneously (e.g. AB blood type)
  • Multiple alleles: more than 2 alleles exist in population (e.g. ABO blood system: Iᴬ, Iᴮ, i)
  • Linked genes: genes on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together
  • Recombination frequency: % recombinant offspring = map units (cM) between genes
  • Gene mapping: recombination frequency used to determine relative positions of genes on a chromosome
  • Chi-square test: compares observed vs. expected ratios to determine if deviation is significant
  • X-linked recessive: more common in males (only one X); carrier females pass it on
  • Examples: colour blindness, hemophilia, Duchenne muscular dystrophy
  • Pedigree analysis: squares = males, circles = females; filled = affected; identify inheritance pattern from pattern of expression
  • Autosomal dominant: appears every generation; both sexes equally affected
  • Autosomal recessive: can skip generations; carriers appear normal

Section 3C — Molecular Genetics

  • DNA structure: double helix; sugar-phosphate backbone; nitrogenous bases (A-T, G-C)
  • Nucleotide: phosphate + deoxyribose sugar + nitrogenous base
  • Semi-conservative replication: each new DNA molecule has one original and one new strand
  • Key enzymes: helicase (unwinds), primase (adds RNA primer), DNA polymerase (adds nucleotides 5'→3'), DNA ligase (joins Okazaki fragments)
  • Transcription: DNA → mRNA; occurs in nucleus; RNA polymerase reads 3'→5' template; mRNA synthesized 5'→3'
  • Translation: mRNA → protein; occurs at ribosomes; tRNA anticodon matches mRNA codon; grows from N to C terminus
  • Codons: 3-base mRNA sequences; 64 possible; AUG = start; UAA, UAG, UGA = stop
  • Central dogma: DNA → RNA → protein (with reverse transcription and RNA replication as exceptions)
  • Point mutations: substitution, insertion, deletion; insertion/deletion cause frameshift
  • Silent, missense, nonsense: same AA / different AA / stop codon introduced
  • Mutagens: UV radiation, X-rays, chemical mutagens (e.g. benzene)
  • Recombinant DNA: restriction enzymes cut DNA; ligase joins fragments; vectors carry insert into host cell
  • PCR: amplifies DNA using primers and thermocycler; denaturation → annealing → extension
  • Gel electrophoresis: separates DNA by size; smaller fragments migrate farther

Theme: Information & Inheritance. Unit C covers how cells divide, how genetic information is organized and passed on, and how DNA is expressed and modified. This is the highest-weighted unit — master the calculations!

🔢 Genetics Problem Strategy

  • Step 1: Determine dominance relationships
  • Step 2: Assign genotypes to parents
  • Step 3: Draw Punnett square
  • Step 4: Determine offspring genotype ratios
  • Step 5: Convert to phenotype ratios
  • Dihybrid: use FOIL or 16-box Punnett

🧬 Protein Synthesis Summary

  • Transcription: nucleus; DNA → mRNA
  • mRNA leaves nucleus through nuclear pore
  • Translation: ribosome; mRNA + tRNA → polypeptide
  • Each codon = 3 bases = 1 amino acid
  • AUG = Met = start codon
  • Ribosome moves 5'→3' along mRNA

📊 Chi-Square Test

  • χ² = Σ[(O − E)² / E]
  • O = observed, E = expected
  • df = (categories − 1)
  • If χ² < critical value → results fit expected ratio
  • If χ² > critical value → significant deviation
  • Use p = 0.05 significance level in Bio 30

🗺️ Gene Linkage & Mapping

  • Genes on same chromosome = linked
  • Recombination frequency = # recombinants / total × 100%
  • 1% recombination = 1 map unit (cM)
  • <50% recombination → genes are linked
  • 50% recombination → genes behave as if on separate chromosomes
  • Three-point crosses confirm gene order

Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium & Population Genetics

  • Gene pool: all alleles in a population at a given time
  • H-W equations: p + q = 1 (allele frequencies); p² + 2pq + q² = 1 (genotype frequencies)
  • p² = AA, 2pq = Aa, q² = aa
  • H-W equilibrium conditions: large population, random mating, no mutation, no migration, no natural selection
  • Solving H-W problems: start with q² (recessive phenotype) → q → p → p², 2pq
  • Natural selection: differential survival and reproduction based on heritable traits
  • Genetic drift: random changes in allele frequency; most significant in small populations
  • Bottleneck effect: population reduced dramatically → loss of genetic diversity
  • Founder effect: new population from few individuals → limited gene pool
  • Gene flow: movement of alleles into or out of population via migration
  • Mutation: ultimate source of new alleles
  • Speciation: formation of new species; requires reproductive isolation
  • Allopatric speciation: geographic isolation → separate gene pools evolve independently → reproductive isolation
  • Population interactions: predation (+/−), competition (−/−), mutualism (+/+), commensalism (+/0), parasitism (+/−)
  • Carrying capacity (K): maximum population size an environment can sustain
  • Logistic growth: S-curve; population grows quickly at first, slows as it approaches K
  • Exponential growth: J-curve; unlimited resources → constant growth rate (r)
  • Ecological succession: pioneer species → intermediate communities → climax community

Theme: Change & Diversity. Unit D bridges genetics and ecology — showing how genetic variation within populations is the raw material for evolution, and how populations interact within ecosystems.

🧮 Hardy-Weinberg Problem Solving

  • Step 1: Identify q² (% showing recessive phenotype)
  • Step 2: q = √q²
  • Step 3: p = 1 − q
  • Step 4: p² = AA, 2pq = Aa, q² = aa
  • Step 5: multiply by population size for actual numbers
  • Check: p + q must = 1; p² + 2pq + q² must = 1

⚡ 5 Forces That Change Allele Frequency

  • 1. Natural selection — non-random survival
  • 2. Genetic drift — random chance (small populations)
  • 3. Gene flow — migration in/out
  • 4. Mutation — new alleles created
  • 5. Non-random mating — changes genotype ratios
  • All 5 violate H-W equilibrium → evolution occurring

📈 Population Growth Curves

  • Exponential (J-curve): dN/dt = rN; unlimited resources
  • Logistic (S-curve): slows as N → K
  • K = carrying capacity (environmental limit)
  • r-strategists: many offspring, little care, unstable environments
  • K-strategists: few offspring, much care, stable environments

🌿 Species Interactions

  • Predation: predator +, prey − (cycles: lynx/hare)
  • Competition: −/− (niche overlap; competitive exclusion)
  • Mutualism: +/+ (bees & flowers; mycorrhizae)
  • Commensalism: +/0 (barnacles on whale)
  • Parasitism: +/− (tapeworm in host)

Exam-Day Strategies

📝 Multiple Choice Tips

  • Read ALL options before choosing
  • Eliminate obviously wrong answers first
  • Watch for "always," "never," "all," "none" — often wrong
  • Diagrams: label all parts before answering questions about them
  • Flag uncertain questions and return at the end
  • Your first instinct is often correct — don't second-guess without reason

✍️ Written Response Tips

  • Read the question carefully — underline key words (describe, explain, compare, evaluate)
  • Plan your answer briefly before writing
  • Use scientific vocabulary — it earns marks
  • For "explain" questions: state the fact AND the reason/mechanism
  • For calculations: show ALL steps; partial marks are awarded
  • Match your response length to the mark value

📊 Diagram Questions

  • Label clearly with proper terminology
  • Draw arrows to show direction of flow/signal
  • Use pencil so you can erase and correct
  • If interpreting a graph: state the trend, give specific values, explain the biology
  • Menstrual cycle graphs: know what each hormone is doing at each phase

🧮 Calculation Checklist

  • Genetics: set up Punnett square first, even if it seems simple
  • Hardy-Weinberg: always start with q² (recessive phenotype)
  • Show p + q = 1 and p² + 2pq + q² = 1 explicitly
  • Check your math: do final values make biological sense?
  • Include units where appropriate
